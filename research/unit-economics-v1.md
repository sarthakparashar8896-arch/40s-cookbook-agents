## Section 1: Assumptions & Methodology

### Volume & Mix
- **Total launch volume:** 5,000 bottles/month (conservative; typical food brand launch)
- **SKU mix assumption:** 60% 250g, 40% 500g (250g drives discovery; 500g drives LTV)
- **Variant mix assumption:** 30% Mango, 25% Mixed, 25% Chili, 20% Lemon (flavour curve typical for Indian pickle)

### GST Treatment
- **GST rate on pickles:** 12% (HSN Code 2001 — vegetables, fruit prepared with vinegar/oil; branded packaged pickles)
- **Approach:** All MRPs are GST-inclusive. Revenue figures are shown both gross (incl. GST) and net (ex-GST). Costs are shown ex-GST (input tax credit offsets output GST for a registered entity). Net GST liability ≈ 12% on value-add only.
- *⚠️ Assumption to validate: Confirm HSN classification with your CA — some oil-based achar may qualify for 5% if sold loose, but branded/packaged pickles are consistently assessed at 12%.*

### Contract Manufacturing
- Costs assume a contract manufacturer in Punjab/Rajasthan/Maharashtra (major pickle CM hubs in India)
- Minimum order quantity: 1,000 kg/batch (realistic for 5,000 bottles/month of 250g = 1,250 kg)
- CM costs include: raw material procurement, processing, oil/spice/salt, labour, QC, packaging into jars

### Pricing Philosophy
- Target positioning: **₹90–₹120/100g** — above FarmDidi (₹92/100g) and comparable to Naagin (₹102/100g)
- Rationale: The 40's Cookbook is a brand-led, design-forward product justifying a premium over functional artisan brands
- Not competing on price with Mother's Recipe (₹19–₹52/100g) or Priya (₹32–₹35/100g)

## Section 10: Revised Pricing Recommendation (Post-Margin Analysis)

The base analysis reveals that **250g at ₹299 is margin-thin on QC and loss-making in modern retail.** The 500g at ₹499 works well across all channels.

### Revised Recommendation

| SKU | Revised MRP | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| All 250g variants | **₹349** | ₹139.6/100g — maintains premium positioning; makes QC viable (25%+ margin); restaurant supply at ₹175 |
| All 500g variants | **₹499–₹549** | ₹499 for Lemon/Mixed; ₹549 for Mango/Chili (justify with more expensive raw material + hero variant premium) |

At ₹349 for 250g:
- D2C margin: ~38% (customer pays shipping)
- Blinkit margin: ~25% ✓
- Nature's Basket margin: ~18% (still thin; use as brand presence, not P&L)
- This positions 250g at a 16% premium to FarmDidi (₹299 for 325g = ₹92/100g; ₹349 for 250g = ₹140/100g)
- Justified by: superior brand design, urban identity, quick-commerce availability, smaller "try-me" pack

**Break-even volume** = Fixed Costs ÷ Contribution Margin = ₹3,00,000 ÷ ₹139 = **≈ 2,160 bottles/month**

> **At 5,000 bottles/month:** Monthly profit = (5,000 × ₹139) − ₹3,00,000 = ₹6,95,000 − ₹3,00,000 = **₹3,95,000/month**  
> Net margin on revenue: ₹3,95,000 / (5,000 × ₹289) = ₹3,95,000 / ₹14,45,000 = **27.3%** ✓

> *Note: Break-even at ~2,160 bottles/month is reassuringly low. Even at 50% of target volume (2,500 bottles), the brand is profitable.*

## Section 14: Strategic Recommendations

### Prioritize 500g SKU from Day 1
The 500g is where all the margin is — D2C 43%, QC 36%, retail 28%. Push the 500g as the hero SKU in all marketing. Use 250g only as a trial/discovery/gifting SKU.

### Lead with D2C, Not Modern Retail
Modern retail at ₹349 MRP for 250g generates near-zero margins. Don't chase Nature's Basket listing in year 1 — build D2C repeat customer base first, then leverage that proof-of-demand to negotiate better retail terms.

### QC Viability Requires ₹349+ MRP on 250g
At ₹299, Blinkit margins are 13% — not sustainable. Either price at ₹349 or accept that QC is a brand-building investment for the first 6 months with thin/zero contribution from that channel.

### Engineer Average Order Value
At D2C, a 2-bottle order (mix of variants) with ₹70 shipping split across 2 units dramatically improves margin. Create "Try 2" bundles at ₹599–649 to drive AOV above free shipping threshold.

### Lock CM Contract with Raw Material Hedge
The biggest cost risk is mango raw material inflation. Negotiate with CM on fixed-price contract for at least the first 12 months, even if it means paying slightly above spot during low season.
